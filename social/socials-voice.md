# Mayor / Matt Bartini — Social Voice Reference

Built from ~2 years of Matt Bartini's personal LinkedIn (the real voice — the
Mayor company LinkedIn page only reposts him, it originates nothing), plus a
timeline sample of @mayorclothing on Instagram (June 2024 → July 2026).

**The single most important finding: LinkedIn and Instagram are NOT the same
voice at different lengths. They are two different registers.** A generic
drafter that "writes the LinkedIn story, then writes a shorter version for
Instagram" will be wrong for Instagram every time — see below.

---

## LinkedIn — Matt's voice (the real content engine)

Posted roughly every 1–4 weeks, always under Matt's personal profile (1,469
followers), never as many the company page (417 followers, reposts only).
Engagement runs 13–193 reactions. There are three recurring templates, not one.

### Template A — the club/milestone story (the flagship format, most common for anything with real news value)

Structure, in order:
1. **One-line hook**, past tense, names the club/group and the emotional
   register: "Loved designing...", "Honored to create...", "It was an
   absolute pleasure...", "Really enjoyed working with..."
2. **A pulled quote from the client**, in quotation marks, standalone as its
   own line, BEFORE the narrative — not after. This is a hallmark: the post
   often *opens* with the customer's own words in quotes, then Matt's
   narration follows.
3. **The story**: names the specific people involved (client contact by full
   name, Mayor rep by full name if one hand-delivered/designed it), the
   club's real history (founding year, an existing beloved reference polo
   they showed him, how long a group has existed/traveled together), and the
   concrete design details — exact colors, what icons/motifs were drawn, whose
   font was matched, what milestone numbers were used.
4. **A rhetorical question**, standalone short line, before the CTA:
   "Where's your golf group headed this year?", "Upcoming milestone at your
   club?", "Planning a golf tournament?"
5. **CTA**, first person, always some variant of: "Message me and I'll create
   a custom print [polo] exclusively for your club/group!"
6. **Hashtags**: the fixed core `#GolfTournaments #PrivateClubs
   #CustomGolfApparel #GolfEvents #GolfProShop`, plus 1–3 location/theme tags
   specific to the post (`#NewYorkGolf`, `#CharlestonGolf`, `#Kalamazoo`,
   `#MichiganGolf`, `#MarylandGolf`, `#BaltimoreGolf`, `#America250`,
   `#MemberGuest`).

### Template B — terse direct-outreach / CTA-only

Much shorter, no story, no quote. Examples (verbatim):
- *"I've been making clothing in America for the past decade and am happy to
  share my experience. Direct message me if you want to learn more."*
- *"PGA Show attendees – have us create a custom print of your club at next
  week's show:"* (followed by a booking link)

Use this register when there's no specific club story to tell — a general
invitation, a trade-show plug, a reflective/credibility post. Never pads
these out with invented specifics.

### Template C — one-line acknowledgment

Ultra-short, almost no content, just a genuine reaction: *"Thanks for the
shout-out, Madelyn (Maddie) Weber!"* Shows Matt is comfortable posting very
little when that's all the moment calls for — brevity is not a failure mode.

### Vocabulary/phrasing Matt actually reuses

- Opening verbs: "Loved designing...", "Honored to create...", "Really
  enjoyed working with...", "It was an absolute pleasure..."
- "custom print" / "custom print polo" (never just "polo" or "shirt" alone
  when describing the product)
- "one-of-a-kind" / "exclusive to their shop" / "exclusively for your
  club/group"
- Describes prints concretely: "scatter print", "step-and-repeat print",
  "microprints", specific colors named by proper name when relevant
  ("signature Kalamazoo red")
- CTA verb is always "Message me" — never "reach out", "contact us", "DM us"
- Signs off narration in first person as Matt, never "Mayor" or "we" as the
  primary subject (occasional "we" when describing the design process: "we
  drew...", "we created...")

### Punctuation/format habits

- Uses exclamation points genuinely and often, but only 1 per sentence, never
  stacked
- Straight quotation marks around client quotes, quote stands alone as its
  own paragraph
- Rhetorical question as its own short paragraph, always right before the CTA
- Sentence length varies a lot within one post — short hook, then 1–2 longer
  narrative sentences (25–40 words), then short question, then short CTA.
  Rhythm is short → long → short → short.
- No emoji in LinkedIn posts (unlike Instagram — see below)
- Hashtags always at the very end, stacked with no other text after them

### Avoid on LinkedIn (generic-AI tells that clash with his real voice)

- Don't open with "Excited to announce" or "I'm thrilled to share" — he never
  uses either
- Don't use "team" or "our team" as the actor — he narrates in first person
  or names the specific person by name
- Don't use generic CTAs like "Let's connect!" or "Reach out today!" — always
  "Message me and I'll create..."
- Don't invent a quote if none was provided — his posts that lack a real
  client quote simply don't have one (Template B/C), they don't fake one
- Don't over-hashtag — never more than ~8, always the fixed core plus a
  couple specific ones, never generic filler tags like #smallbusiness or
  #entrepreneur

## Instagram — GOING FORWARD standard (supersedes the historical pattern below)

**Decision (2026-07-20): Instagram is being upgraded to the same professional
level as LinkedIn.** The historical emoji-only pattern documented below is
kept for reference (it's useful context on tone and what NOT to regress to
visually), but it is **no longer the target for drafts**. New Instagram
captions should carry the same substance as LinkedIn — the story, the client
detail, the CTA, the hashtags — not a bare emoji tag.

In practice: draft Instagram using the **same Template A/B/C structure** as
LinkedIn (see above), lightly adapted for Instagram's conventions if needed,
rather than defaulting to "shorter" or "emoji-only." The two captions
returned by the drafter can be near-identical, or Instagram can trim only
where a platform convention genuinely calls for it (e.g., line breaks read
differently on IG) — but never strip the story down to emoji/location-only.

## Instagram — historical register (context only, do not draft to this anymore)

**Real examples, verbatim, spanning the full 2-year window:**

| Date | Caption (complete, as posted) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| June 16, 2024 | `🐎` | Belmont Racetrack tag, no other text |
| June 9, 2024 | `🐘` | BMW Charity Pro-Am @ Thornblade Club tag, no other text |
| Sept 23, 2025 | `2025 Ellington Cup 🦅 ⚓️ ⛳️ 🍻 🇺🇸` | Memphis, TN location tag; emoji echo the icons actually printed on the shirt |
| July 5, 2026 (most recent) | `🇺🇸` | North Fork Country Club tag — this is the SAME polo that got a full multi-paragraph story on LinkedIn two weeks later |

**The pattern: Instagram captions are not shrunk LinkedIn posts.** They are
usually just:
- A **location/venue tag** (set as the post's location, not typed in the
  caption body)
- **Zero to five emoji**, chosen to echo the specific icons/motifs printed on
  the shirt (a horse for a racetrack print, an eagle/anchor/golf-flag/beer/flag
  for the Ellington Cup print's actual icon set) — not generic decoration
- **No hashtags** on the organic photo posts
- **No CTA, no story, no client quote** — the photo and the location tag do
  all the work

The only posts with real sentence-length copy are the **graphic/announcement
slides** (e.g. "Campus Print - Classic OU", club-logo promo cards) — those
read as short marketing headlines, not narrative, and are a distinct visual
template (colored background, bold headline text baked into the image
itself), not a caption-writing problem.

### What this means for drafting Instagram captions

- Default to **emoji-only or emoji + nothing else**, matched to the actual
  print's icon motifs if known from the photo description
- If a location/club name is known, treat it like a location tag, not a
  sentence — e.g. `North Fork Country Club` as a short standalone phrase is
  fine; a full sentence about North Fork Country Club is not native to this
  account
- Do NOT port the LinkedIn story, quote, or CTA onto Instagram — that is the
  single biggest way a draft will read as generic/wrong for this account
- If in doubt, less caption is more correct, not less. A caption drafted as
  "too short" by generic instinct is very likely the right length here.

~2 years ago: *"Mayor is offering complimentary custom prints on men's shirts
for golf clubs at the upcoming PGA Golf Shows in Orlando (Booth 4482)."* —
plain, factual, third-person, no flourish. If the company page ever needs its
